# Cross-Linguistic Study: How Languages Shape Root Recovery

## Introduction

Translating the same Arabic roots into six languages reveals how each language's structure shapes what the translation can express. The root is constant; the linguistic vessel changes. This document catalogues what we learned about each language's strengths, limitations, and unique contributions to the project.

## The Central Axis: k-f-r and s-l-m Across Languages

| Language | k-f-r (conceal) | s-l-m (devote/submit) | Observation |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------|-------------|
| English | conceal / deny / reject / ungrateful | devoted / submit / surrender / at peace | Rich synonym palette; KJV register provides sacred gravity |
| Portuguese | ocultar / negar / rejeitar / ingrato | Devotos / submeter-se | Natural ambiguity tolerance; subjunctive mood holds multiple meanings simultaneously |
| Dutch | verhullen / loochenen / verzaken / ondankbaar | Toegewijden / onderwerpen | "Verhullen" carries moral weight (deliberate veiling); Statenvertaling register adds solemnity |

| Frisian | ferbergje / lechenje / fersaakje | de Tawijden / jin ûnderwerpje | Closest to English; cognates visible across both translations |

## Language-Specific Insights

### English: The Archaic Register Advantage

KJV English (thee, thy, ye, hath) isn't merely stylistic — it's structurally necessary. Arabic is particle-heavy and paratactic ("and... and... then... and..."). Modern English resists this; KJV English embraces it. "And" at the start of a sentence is natural in KJV. "Verily" maps onto Arabic "inna" without strain. The archaic register gives English permission to be structurally faithful.

The English translation also benefits from being the first — every root discovery was made in English first, then carried to other languages. This means the English commentary is deepest, but it also means English carries interpretive decisions that the other languages could challenge.

### Portuguese: The Ambiguity Language

Portuguese emerged as the fastest and most natural translation. Key reasons:

- **Subjunctive richness**: Portuguese has a full subjunctive system that holds ambiguity naturally. Where English forces "if he does" or "if he did" (choosing a reality level), Portuguese can say "se ele fizer" and leave the reality open.
- **Formal register alive**: The "vos/vosso" register is still used in Portugal (and liturgically in Brazil), making the sacred register natural rather than artificially archaic.
- **Latin-Arabic bridge**: Portuguese absorbed significant Arabic vocabulary during the Moorish period, giving it natural comfort with Arabic-derived concepts — but through the ROOT meanings, not the later Islamic theological overlay.

The Portuguese crucifixion verse (4:157) landed as "foi-lhes tornado ambiguo" — "it was made ambiguous unto them" — which may be more natural in Portuguese than in any other language, because Portuguese *thinks* in ambiguity.

### Dutch: The Moral Weight Language

Dutch forced the sharpest translation choices:

- **"Verhullen"** for k-f-r — not neutral "verbergen" (hide) but morally charged "verhullen" (to veil deliberately, to cloak). Dutch demanded a word that carried judgment.
- **"Verborgenen"** for jinn — the hidden ones. This creates a visible root web: verborgenen (hidden beings) / verborgen Rijk (hidden realm) / verhullen (to veil) — three Dutch words mapping the Arabic concealment theme.
- **Statenvertaling register**: The gij/uw forms are more archaic in Dutch than thee/thy is in English. Most Dutch speakers encounter this register only in the Bible. Using it for the Qur'an makes a theological statement: this text deserves the same dignity.

### Esperanto: The Morphological Mirror

Esperanto's constructed morphology mirrors Arabic's root system more than any natural language:

- **mal-kashi** (un-conceal) = revelation. The Esperanto word for divine disclosure literally contains the negation of k-f-r. God "malkashas" (un-conceals) while humans "kashas" (conceal). No other language in the project encodes the theology this precisely in the word structure.
- **Bonlingvismo challenge**: LLMs tend to use Esperanto with full morphological creativity, producing grammatically correct but unusual compounds. Native Esperanto speakers use a more conservative vocabulary. The translation must balance expressiveness with readability.
- **No archaic register**: Esperanto has no KJV equivalent — no old/new distinction in the language. This means the sacred register must come from vocabulary choice and sentence rhythm, not from grammatical forms.
- **Supersigned characters**: The diacriticals (ch, gh, hh, jh, sh, uh) add a production burden but also give the text a visual distinctiveness that marks it as "other" — appropriate for sacred text.

### Indonesian: The Loanword Problem

Indonesian presents a unique challenge that no other language in the project faces:

- **Arabic loanwords carry conventional meanings**: Words like "kafir," "muslim," "jin," "surga" are already Indonesian words — but they carry the received theological meanings, not the root meanings. The root-recovery translation must deliberately AVOID these familiar words and replace them with native Indonesian equivalents.
- **"Menyembunyikan" instead of "kafir"**: This forces Indonesian readers to actually think about what the Arabic means, rather than importing the label.
- **"Bacaan" instead of "Al-Qur'an"**: Translating the name of the Book itself is more radical in Indonesian than in any other language, because "Al-Qur'an" is an everyday Indonesian word. Calling it "Bacaan" (the Reading/Recitation) is defamiliarizing in a productive way.
- **Register exists**: Formal Indonesian (the "engkau" register, literary prose style) provides the sacred weight that some other languages lack.

### Frisian: The English Cognate

Frisian is the closest living relative to English, and translating the Qur'an into both languages side by side reveals their common ancestry:

- **Foardracht / Recitation**: cognate pair (both from "speaking forth")
- **ferbergje / conceal**: the Frisian "fer-" prefix parallels English "for-" (as in "forbid")
- **Weromkearden / Returners**: "werom" = "back" (cf. English "were-" in "werewolf" = man-wolf)
- **Tun / Garden**: Frisian preserves the old Germanic word for enclosed garden

The Frisian translation serves as a living bridge between the English translation and the Germanic root system — showing that the Arabic roots can be carried through any language family.

## The Register Spectrum

From most formal/archaic to most accessible:

1. **Dutch** (Statenvertaling gij/uw) — most archaic, least accessible to modern speakers
2. **English** (KJV thee/thy) — archaic but widely recognized as sacred register
3. **Portuguese** (vos/vosso) — formal but still living in Portugal
4. **Indonesian** (formal engkau register) — formal but natural
5. **Esperanto** (standard literary) — no archaic register available; dignity through vocabulary
6. **Frisian** (literary Standertfrysk) — small literary tradition; formal but not archaic

The ideal translation register for each language is the one that says: "this text is sacred, but you can still read it." Too archaic and readers bounce. Too modern and the gravity is lost. Each language found its own balance.

## The Col Exception

Col (South Sumatra, endangered) was the only language where the project encountered an absolute barrier: the language lacks a formal/sacred register entirely. An attempt to translate Al-Fatihah in Col "sounded rude" (per native speaker Valent Valegas). The eventual translation, done by Valegas himself, found a workable middle ground — but confirmed that not every language can carry every text. Register is not optional for scripture.

However, the Col Al-Fatihah may be the first Qur'anic text ever written in this endangered language — making it significant for language preservation even if the register remains informal.

## Conclusion

The Arabic root system is universal — every language we tested could carry the root meanings. But each language carries them differently: through moral weight (Dutch), natural ambiguity (Portuguese), morphological mirroring (Esperanto), defamiliarization (Indonesian), ancestral cognates (Frisian), or archaic gravity (English).

The root is constant. The vessel shapes the light. Same sun, six prisms, six spectra — all true, all partial, all pointing back to the source.
